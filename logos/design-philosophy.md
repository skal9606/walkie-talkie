# Tropical Signal

## Design Philosophy

A visual language for an app that teaches a language through voice. The logo system lives at the intersection of two objects: the retro handheld radio, and the tropical evening light of Rio. Mint and coral against near-black — the palette of a dusk conversation, half-overheard across an open window.

Form is confident and reductive. Every curve is the minimum curve required; every line holds its weight. Shapes are built from perfect geometry — circles, arcs, rounded rectangles — aligned on an invisible grid that a designer would feel before they saw. The work reads as the output of hundreds of small, deliberate adjustments: a corner radius eased until it settles, a stroke weight tuned against an adjacent void until the whole composition clicks.

Space is generous but not empty. Each mark earns its place. Negative space is treated as a positive material, cut with the same care as the shapes themselves. Scale is chosen for silhouette recognition: at 16px in a browser tab, at 1024px on a billboard, the mark still holds.

Color is a duet, never a choir. Mint carries voice — soft, instructive, patient. Coral carries warmth — urgency, signal, the human pulse underneath the teaching. The two meet but never muddy. Where they overlap, the meeting is handled with the precision of a master printer registering two-plate silkscreen.

Typography, when present, is structural. It does not decorate; it joins the geometry. Letters are set with the same care as the graphic elements — tracking tuned, weight paired to the surrounding shapes, no flourish unjustified.

The result should feel inevitable — as though each mark had always existed and the designer simply uncovered it. Meticulously crafted. Painstakingly refined. The product of deep expertise in the reductive tradition: Vignelli, Müller-Brockmann, Brazilian modernism. Nothing extra. Nothing missing.
